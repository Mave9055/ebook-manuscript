# ARTICLE 8: NORMAL IS THE MOST ADDICTIVE STATE
**Addicts chase normal, not highs.**

When people think of addiction, they think of the rush. The euphoria. The high. They think of people chasing a feeling of being better than everyone else. But for me, and for many people with trauma histories, it was never about the high. It was about the quiet.

The first time I took a hydrocodone, I didn't feel high. I didn't feel numb. I felt normal. My heart stopped racing. My jaw unclenched. The constant static in my head — the buzzing, the scanning, the dread — it just… quieted. For the first time in my life, I could breathe. That feeling — normal — was more addictive than any high I ever chased.

**The Baseline Nobody Talks About**

Most people don't know what it's like to have a nervous system that treats safety as a threat. They don't wake up already triggered. They don't scan every room for exits. They have a baseline of calm. Their alarm only goes off when there's actually danger.

I never had that. My baseline was panic. The NICU wired me for high alert. The violence at six taught me that stillness equals survival. By the time I was a teenager, my nervous system was in a constant state of emergency. Fluorescent lights felt like interrogation. Crowds felt like ambushes. Silence felt like the calm before the next disaster.

Normal, for me, was a war zone.

So when I found a pill that turned the volume down — that made me feel what other people probably felt every day — it wasn't about getting high. It was about finally, briefly, being able to exist without the alarm blaring. Research on addiction and self-medication has consistently shown that substance use is not a choice but a desperate attempt to solve the problem of unbearable pain. For me, the unbearable pain wasn't just emotional. It was physiological. It was a nervous system that never learned what safe felt like.

**The Longing for Quiet**

At seventeen, a migraine knocked me flat. My mother handed me a pill. Thirty minutes later, I was at the sink scrubbing dishes like I was reborn. Not because I was high. Because the noise in my head had stopped. The constant, grinding, exhausting noise of hypervigilance — the scanning, the bracing, the waiting for the next bad thing — it just went quiet.

That's what I became addicted to. Not the rush. The quiet. Research on addiction notes that it is often about relief — relief from pain, from stress, from the intolerable state of being. For people with trauma histories, that intolerable state is the baseline. The substance doesn't create a high. It creates a temporary normal. And when you've never known normal, that feeling is more valuable than anything.

**The Trap of Chasing Normal**

Here's the trap. The substance works — at first. It turns down the volume. Gives you a few hours of quiet. But then it wears off. The alarm comes back louder than before. Your nervous system has adapted. It overshoots. Now you need more to get the same effect. And more. And more.

That's not weakness. That's physiology. That's tolerance. But the shame narrative says: *See? You're out of control. You're an addict.* No. I was chasing the feeling of being normal. And the closer I got, the further it slipped away.

Research is clear: most people who use substances do so to cope with distress, not to pursue pleasure. The majority of addiction is rooted in social and psychological suffering, not the pharmacology of the drug itself. It's not the substance that's the problem. It's the storm the person is trying to survive.

**The Crash After the Quiet**

When the substance stopped working — when tolerance made the quiet impossible to find — I switched to stay afloat, not to get higher. Meth kept me upright when grief would have put me on the floor. It made me functional long enough to pretend I was okay. But pretending isn't living. And the crash always came.

The crash was worse than the original pain. Because now, on top of the alarm, there was shame. *You needed this. You're weak. You can't handle life.* And the worst part? The shame was right about one thing. I couldn't handle life like a person whose nervous system had been wired for peace. I was a person whose nervous system had been wired for war before I had words for it. Normal was never available to me. I was just trying to fake it.

**What I Needed Wasn't a High — It Was a New Baseline**

I didn't need more willpower. I needed a nervous system that didn't treat Tuesday like a threat. I didn't need to be told to just stop. I needed to be shown how to regulate.

True change wasn't about finding a better volume dial. It was about rewriting the score. Not how to abstain — but how to regulate. How to find safety in your own skin. How to build a baseline that doesn't require a chemical crutch. I'm not there yet. I'm aligned, not healed. But I've stopped chasing normal through a pipe. Now I'm chasing it through breath, through grounding, through understanding. It's slower. It's harder. But it's real.

When the pull comes — the urge toward the thing that used to turn down the volume — I don't shame myself. I put my hand on my chest and whisper: *I'm not chasing a high. I'm chasing normal. That's not weakness. That's a nervous system asking for peace.* You can borrow my words if you need them.

The storm wired us for danger. I wasn't chasing a high — I was chasing normal. And normal was a foreign country.
