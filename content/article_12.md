# ARTICLE 12: HEALING IS A SPIRAL
**Setbacks are not resets.**

I used to measure my recovery like a stock chart. Up was good. Down was failure. A straight line from broken to fixed — that's what I thought healing was supposed to look like. Every relapse, every freeze, every missed court date felt like a crash. And I would look at the wreckage and say: *See? You haven't made any progress. You're right back where you started.* I was wrong. Not about the pain of falling. About the shape of healing.

Healing isn't a straight line. It's a spiral.

You pass the same wounds. The same triggers. The same feelings. But each time, you're at a different level — with more awareness, better tools, less shame. Setbacks are not resets. They're just the next loop of the spiral.

**The Myth of the Straight Line**

Society loves the redemption arc. The addict who gets clean and stays clean. The trauma survivor who "overcomes" their past and never looks back. It's a satisfying story. It's also a lie. Real healing doesn't move in straight lines. It loops back. It revisits old pain. It has setbacks that feel like failures but are actually part of the process.

One of the most damaging lies we tell ourselves is that healing should be linear — that if we're doing it right, we should only go forward, never back. That lie nearly killed me. Every time I relapsed, I believed I had erased all my progress. Every time I froze in a parking lot, I believed I was back at square one. But I had changed. I just couldn't see it. Because I was measuring with the wrong ruler.

**The Spiral vs. The Line**

A straight line says: from broken to fixed. Once you reach a point, you stay there. If you fall, you start over. A spiral says: you pass the same point on the circle — but at a different height. Each time you encounter the trigger, the feeling, the situation, you bring more awareness. More tools. Less shame. The wound doesn't disappear. But your capacity to hold it grows.

Research on trauma recovery describes this as a pendulating process — moving between activation and resolution, revisiting the same material from different angles. Not a straight line. A rhythm. Addiction research also notes that relapse is a common feature of recovery, not a sign that it failed. Relapse means the condition is persistent — and that recovery requires ongoing adjustment, not a one-time fix.

I didn't know the terminology back then. I just knew that every time I fell, I learned something. Not about willpower — about triggers, patterns, what my system needed.

**The Loops I Kept Revisiting**

I've frozen in parking lots more times than I can count. The first time, I didn't even know what was happening. I called myself lazy. Weak. A failure. The tenth time, I could name it. *That's freeze. My system is trying to survive.* The hundredth time, I had a tool. A breath. A phrase. A crack in the loop. Same parking lot. Same frozen legs. Same racing heart. But I wasn't the same person. I had more awareness. Better tools. Less shame.

That's the spiral. Same wound, better tools.

The same happened with addiction. I relapsed more times than I can count. Each time, the shame was brutal. But each time, I learned something. The relapse wasn't a reset. It was data. A clue about what I still needed. Research on relapse describes it as a process, not an event — one that builds over time through stress and depletion. Recognizing that process allows you to interrupt it earlier next time. That's not failure. That's learning.

**Why Setbacks Feel Like Starting Over**

Setbacks feel catastrophic because we measure them against the straight line. If you believe you're supposed to only go forward, any backward movement feels like falling off a cliff. But on a spiral, backward movement isn't falling. It's revisiting.

I used to believe that a relapse meant I had learned nothing. That a freeze meant I was still the six-year-old under the blanket. Now I know: the relapse means I need to look at a different part of the spiral. The freeze means my body is still learning. The rage means the grief is still there, asking to be seen. These aren't proof you're broken. They're data points. Opportunities to practice getting back up.

**How I Learned to Spiral Instead of Crash**

I didn't learn this from a book. I learned it from falling so many times that I finally noticed I was getting back up faster. The first relapse, I stayed down for months. The second, weeks. The third, days. The fourth, hours. The shame didn't disappear. But it lost some of its power. Because I had evidence: I had been here before — and I got up.

That's the spiral. Not the absence of falls. The shortening of the time between them.

Research on resilience and the nervous system supports this: the body learns to recover through repeated experiences of falling and getting back up. Each repetition strengthens the pathways of recovery. I felt that happening, even when I couldn't explain it.

**What I Wish I Had Known**

I wish someone had told me, years ago, that setbacks were not resets. That the spiral was normal. That the shame of falling was more dangerous than the fall itself. I wish someone had given me a different ruler. Not *how long since my last relapse* — but *how quickly did I reach for a tool when the alarm went off?*

You don't heal by fixing the past. You heal by upgrading the present wiring.

The past doesn't change. But your relationship to it can. The spiral brings you back to the same memories, the same triggers — but you're not the same person who experienced them the first time. You have more awareness. More tools. Less shame. That's not failure. That's healing.

If you've just fallen — relapsed, frozen, raged, disappeared — hear this. You are not back at zero. You are on the spiral. You've been here before — and you got up then. You can get up now. The shame will tell you otherwise. The shame is a liar.

When it comes — when I look at a setback and believe it erases everything I've learned — I stop. I breathe. And I say it soft: *This is not a reset. This is a spiral. I've been here before — and I got up then. I can get up now.* You can borrow my words if you need them.

The storm wired us for danger. Healing isn't a straight line — it's a spiral. Setbacks are not resets. I've been here before — and I got up then.
