# ARTICLE 1: THE RESCUE BOAT
**Addiction is survival, not weakness.**

For forty-three years, I believed I was an addict. That was the label. That was the verdict. Doctors gave me other labels too — bipolar, borderline, depressed — but the one that burned deepest was *addict*. I believed it because everyone around me believed it. My ex-wife said I chose drugs over my kids. The system said I was noncompliant, self-destructive, unwilling to change. I said it to myself in the dark: *You're weak. You're selfish. You'd rather get high than be a father.*

Here's what I know now, after digging through the wreckage of my own nervous system. I wasn't choosing drugs over my family. I was choosing not to die — with the only shitty tools I had.

**The Question Nobody Asks**

Society loves to blame the boat. "You're just an addict." "Weak." "Selfish." "Why can't you just stop?" But nobody asks why the boat launched in the first place. Nobody looks at the storm that made the water so rough you needed anything — *anything* — to keep from drowning.

My storm started before I had words for it. I was born three pounds, four ounces, dropped into a glass box of wires and alarms. Thirty days in an incubator. No skin-to-skin. Just machines and a priest giving me last rites. My body learned distance before my mind knew the word. It learned that danger is normal. That peace is the exception. That the alarm never shuts off.

Then came the blanket night. Age six, huddled under a thin blanket while my mother got beaten. If I moved, I'd be next. That lesson got written into my nervous system like a line of code: *freeze keeps you alive.* Don't move. Don't cry. Don't exist.

By the time I was a teenager, my baseline wasn't calm — it was panic. My heart raced in quiet rooms. My jaw clenched for no reason. I scanned every room for exits. Fluorescent lights buzzed like NICU monitors inside my skull.

That's not anxiety in the manageable way people talk about it. That's a nervous system carved by repeated, inescapable threat. Research on early adversity — including foundational work on how childhood environments recalibrate the stress response for life — shows that the body keeps the score. Adversity retunes the nervous system to treat friends as enemies. Trauma literally rearranges the brain's wiring.

When that alarm won't shut off — when you can't take another hour of the screaming in your head — you reach for anything that dials it down long enough to breathe. That's the rescue boat.

**The Rescue Boat Is Not the Enemy**

At sixteen, I found alcohol. It dulled the shakes. Made me loose. Gave me a few hours of quiet inside my own skull. At seventeen, a migraine sent me reaching for a pill. Hydrocodone. Thirty minutes later, I was at the sink scrubbing dishes like I was reborn. Not high. Not euphoric. Just… normal. And normal was the most addictive state I'd ever chased — because my baseline had never been anything close to it.

By nineteen, I wasn't using to escape. I was using to operate. Pills got me through double shifts. Meth kept me upright when grief would have put me on the floor. I wasn't living. I was running a machine on borrowed fuel.

You don't choose addiction. You choose the nearest thing that turns down the volume.

The substance isn't the disease. It's a symptom. A desperate, clumsy, dangerous attempt to regulate a nervous system that never learned what safety felt like. The research backs this — the self-medication hypothesis, peer survival studies on how the body stores stress, the landmark ACE study linking childhood adversity to addiction outcomes. You don't need the footnotes to know it's true. You've lived it. Every time you picked up, every time you reached for something to turn down the noise — that wasn't weakness. That was your nervous system screaming: *I want to live. I just don't know how anymore.*

Does that excuse the damage I caused? No. I hurt my kids. I burned bridges. I own that. But calling addiction a moral failure doesn't help. It just piles shame on top of the fire. And shame makes everything worse.

**The Shame Spiral**

Here's what happens when you treat the rescue boat as the enemy. You use. You feel relief. The alarm quiets for a few hours. Then the substance wears off. The alarm comes back louder — because your nervous system has been suppressed, not healed. And on top of the original pain, you now have shame. *You're weak. You're an addict. You chose this.*

The shame is so heavy that the only thing you know to do is use again. Just to get through the night. Just to stop the screaming for one more hour. That's the spiral. And it kills people slowly, quietly, one relapse at a time.

I've buried friends who got caught in that spiral. They weren't bad people. They were drowning in a storm they never asked for, and the shame told them they deserved it.

**What Changes When You Ask a Different Question**

For years I asked: *Why am I so fucked up?* That question is shame-based. It assumes brokenness. It looks for evidence to confirm the guilty verdict.

Then someone who'd been through the same wiring asked me something different: *What happened to you?* I started asking a different question myself: *How did I survive this wiring long enough to still be here?* That question changed everything.

I wasn't an addict because I was weak. I was an addict because my nervous system was screaming for relief and I had no tools, no map, no manual. The substance was the only thing I'd found that could turn down the volume. That's not a moral failure. That's a predictable response to an impossible situation.

The rescue boat was never the enemy. I still fight the urge to reach for something when the alarm blares. Some days I win. Some days I don't. But I don't call myself a failure anymore. I call myself a machine still learning new code. And if you're still reaching for your own rescue boat, hear this: you're not weak. You're trying to live. The only thing missing is other tools.

When the urge hits — the pull toward the thing that quiets the noise — don't fight it with shame. Name it soft, so only you can hear: *That's my nervous system asking for peace. I can find other tools.* You can borrow my words if you need them.

The storm wired us for danger. The fire taught us to burn. But the boat? It was never the enemy. It was proof I still wanted to live.
