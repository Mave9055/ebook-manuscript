# ARTICLE 11: YOU CAN'T OUTRUN YOUR NERVOUS SYSTEM
**The fire follows you. You have to face it.**

I've moved more times than I can count. Clarksville to Little Rock. Little Rock to North Dakota. Back to Arkansas. Different apartments, different jobs, different states. Every time, I told myself the same story: *This time will be different. A fresh start. Leave the old me behind.* But I never left me behind. Because the problem wasn't the place. It was the wiring.

You can't outrun a nervous system. You just give it new terrain to panic on.

**The Geography of Escape**

When my marriage finally collapsed and the kids were gone, I needed distance. Somewhere far enough away that the noise might finally stop. So I went north. Williston, North Dakota. Long hours. Cold air. Structure. I told myself it was a fresh start. The fire came with me.

I didn't feel it at first. The novelty of a new place, the distraction of sixteen-hour shifts, the praise from coworkers — all of it masked the truth. But the fire was there. It just had new fuel. The move north didn't extinguish the fire. It isolated it. I was surrounded by people and completely alone.

Research on trauma and the body is consistent: the nervous system remembers long after the event is over. Moving doesn't reset the score. It just changes the wallpaper.

**The Myth of the Fresh Start**

We love the idea of the fresh start. A new city. A new job. A new relationship. Leave the wreckage behind and become someone new. It's a seductive idea. It's also a trap. Because you can't leave your nervous system at the baggage claim. The wiring you built in childhood — the patterns of freeze, hypervigilance, shame — they're not tied to a zip code. They're tied to your biology.

Research on addiction and trauma has shown consistently that moving doesn't solve the pain. It just gives you a new place to feel it.

**North Dakota: The Fire Followed**

I worked like five people. Learned every position. Took every extra task. My coworkers called me a miracle. From the outside, it looked like I had finally gotten my life together. But inside, I was still on fire. The grief over my mother's death, the guilt over my brother, the shame of my failed marriage — none of it had been processed. It had just been buried under sixteen-hour shifts.

The work wasn't healing. It was hiding. Burnout doesn't announce itself. It creeps in quietly. One morning I was scheduled for a 5 a.m. shift. I woke to pounding on my apartment window. Sunlight everywhere. It was 9 a.m. Shame hit like a gut punch. The fresh start had failed. Because the fresh start was never the solution.

**What's Actually Following You**

You can't outrun your nervous system because your nervous system is not a memory. It's the operating system your body learned before you had words for it. The NICU didn't just keep you alive. It taught you that danger is the baseline. The violence didn't just hurt you. It taught you that stillness equals survival. The losses didn't just grieve you. They taught you that tragedy is the pattern.

These are not ideas. They are physiological adaptations. Your brainstem doesn't care what state you live in. It cares about one thing: keeping you alive. And it learned that the way to do that was to stay on high alert, to freeze at the first sign of threat, to scan every room for exits.

Your nervous system is constantly scanning for cues of safety or danger — below conscious awareness, in every environment, regardless of zip code.

**The Only Thing That Changes**

Sometimes a change of environment is necessary — leaving an abusive relationship, getting out of a dangerous neighborhood, finding a place with fewer triggers. Those moves help because they remove external threats. They don't work because they change your internal wiring.

The only thing that actually changes the wiring is repetition. Safety practiced over time. Steady connection experienced again and again. New patterns layered on top of the old ones until the old ones weaken.

The brain is capable of change throughout life — that's established science. But change requires repetition. It requires new experiences that contradict the old predictions. Moving doesn't do that. Moving gives you a blank slate to repeat the same patterns on. What does change the wiring: sitting in a room with a steady person and not running. Using a breath instead of a substance. Naming the freeze instead of hiding from it. Showing up even when every fiber wants to disappear. Those small repetitions — done again and again — are what actually change the code.

**What I Learned When I Stopped Running**

I didn't stop moving by accident. I stopped because I ran out of places to go. North Dakota didn't work. Rehab didn't work. A new relationship didn't work. The only variable left was me. So I stopped running. I sat in the fire. I let the alarm blare without numbing it. I froze in parking lots and waited for it to pass. I felt the shame and named it instead of hiding from it. It was brutal. It still is. But something shifted.

The storm wasn't outside. It was me. Once I realized that, I stopped trying to outrun myself. I started trying to understand myself. The map. The wiring. The tools. I'm not healed. I'm aligned. And alignment is enough to keep walking.

When I stopped running, I didn't get stuck. I got free. Not free from the alarm — the alarm still blares. But free from the illusion that somewhere else would be better.

I still feel the pull sometimes. The itch to pack a bag, to burn the bridges, to start somewhere new. When it comes, I put my hand on my chest and whisper: *Running won't work. What does my system need right here?* You can borrow my words if you need them.

The storm wired us for danger. I moved to North Dakota. The fire followed. The only way out is through.
